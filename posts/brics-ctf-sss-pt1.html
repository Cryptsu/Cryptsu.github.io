Intro

Once in a while, there comes a problem that makes you more conscious about your insignificant intelligence... Today, it comes in the form of a CTF challenge :'<<

Basically, there's this challenge:

  • generates a random polynomial f(x)f(x)f(x) with N=N=N= 20000156 terms in Fp\mathbb{F}_{p}Fp​, where p=p=p= 20000159,
  • evaluates the outputs f(0),f(1),...,f(p−1)f(0), f(1), ..., f(p-1)f(0),f(1),...,f(p−1) except for f(69),f(420)f(69), f(420)f(69),f(420) and f(1337)f(1337)f(1337),
  • gives us said outputs,
  • gives us the FLAG XORed with the sha512() hash of f(x)f(x)f(x)'s coefficients array.

Trying stuffs...

Since it's hard to crack sha512(), or reverse XOR function with 0 knowleges about one of its inputs, our only route is to recover f(x)f(x)f(x) from its outputs.

Lagrange Interpolation

So, if you're a noob like me, your first thought will probably be:

"Well, this is a piece of cake.

We have 200001562000015620000156 outputs, the polynomial has 200001562000015620000156 terms. I'll just use the Lagrange Interpolation. They have this fancy math that computes a polynomial's NNN coefficients from its NNN evaluations.

We meet all the requirements needed to carry out the computation."


So you gleefully copy an implementation from the Internet, adding inputs, modify stuffs, typing, thinking to yourself: "EZ".

You run the code and wonder why it takes so long. Maybe the output array is too big and needed some time to load? No, it had already been done! You throw a tqdm bar into the main loop of the code and see that it estimates the algorithm will be running for another 7000 hours.


Shit 💩.

You dig into the implementation of Lagrange Interpolation, and realize that given a polynomial with NNN terms:

  • it computes the sum of NNN terms,
  • each term is the product of smaller N−1N-1N−1 linear polynomials.

An O(N2)O(N^2)O(N2) runtime...

This algorithm works fast for small polynomial sizes, like 2 or 3 and some numbers close to it like 10000, but not 20000156. This is when you GASP: "This is not Crypto, this is some Codeforces obsure 200+ IQ optimization problem!!" You know you suck, but you still want try solving a coding optimization stuffs anyways, to see where it goes. Oh boy how shitty you're going feel afterwards!

Fast Fourier Transform

Fast Fourier Transform, or FFT for short, is an algorithm that also can do the convert coefficients -> values thingy. However, this algorithm has a restriction:

The inputs to f(x)f(x)f(x) must be ωN0,ωN1,...,ωNN−1\omega_N^0, \omega_N^1, ..., \omega_N^{N-1}ωN0​,ωN1​,...,ωNN−1​, instead of arbitrary values x0,x1,...,xN−1x_0, x_1, ..., x_{N-1}x0​,x1​,...,xN−1​, where ωN\omega_NωN​ is any NNN-th root of unity (except 1 to avoid ωN0≠ωN1≠...≠ωNN−1\omega_N^0 \neq \omega_N^1 \neq ... \neq \omega_N^{N-1}ωN0​=ωN1​=...=ωNN−1​).

Hmm.. Actually that's what I understand about this...

The actual purpose of FFT seems to be some values -> frequency thingy conversion stuffs. But in this case, it still true (?) idk... math is weird... Maybe I'm wrong on something @.@

But if you want to know the actual definition of FFT, you can read it here: Wiki


To understand why FFT does this, let's formulize things.

Same as before, this relationship between coefficients and values can be represented as the product of a Vandermonde matrix with the coefficient vector f→\overrightarrow{f}f​:

[ωN0ωN0ωN0...ωN0ωN0ωN0ωN1ωN2...ωNN−2ωNN−1ωN0ωN2ωN4...ωN2N−4ωN2N−2...ωN0ωNN−1ωN2(N−1)...ωN(N−2)(N−1)ωN(N−1)(N−1)][f0f1f2...fN−2fN−1]=[f(ωN0)f(ωN1)f(ωN2)...f(ωNN−2)f(ωNN−1)]\begin{bmatrix} \omega_N^0 & \omega_N^0 & \omega_N^0 & ... & \omega_N^0 & \omega_N^0 \\ \omega_N^0 & \omega_N^1 & \omega_N^2 & ... & \omega_N^{N-2} & \omega_N^{N-1} \\ \omega_N^0 & \omega_N^2 & \omega_N^4 & ... & \omega_N^{2N-4} & \omega_N^{2N-2} \\ ... \\ \omega_N^0 & \omega_N^{N-1} & \omega_N^{2(N-1)} & ... & \omega_N^{(N-2)(N-1)} & \omega_N^{(N-1)(N-1)} \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} f_0 \\ f_1 \\ f_2 \\ ... \\ f_{N-2} \\ f_{N-1} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} f(\omega_N^0) \\ f(\omega_N^1) \\ f(\omega_N^2) \\ ... \\ f(\omega_N^{N-2}) \\ f(\omega_N^{N-1}) \end{bmatrix}​ωN0​ωN0​ωN0​...ωN0​​ωN0​ωN1​ωN2​ωNN−1​​ωN0​ωN2​ωN4​ωN2(N−1)​​............​ωN0​ωNN−2​ωN2N−4​ωN(N−2)(N−1)​​ωN0​ωNN−1​ωN2N−2​ωN(N−1)(N−1)​​​​f0​f1​f2​...fN−2​fN−1​​​=​f(ωN0​)f(ωN1​)f(ωN2​)...f(ωNN−2​)f(ωNN−1​)​​

Let's rewrite the equation into: DωN f→=yωN→D_{\omega_N} \text{ } \overrightarrow{f} = \overrightarrow{y_{\omega_N}}DωN​​ f​=yωN​​​, where:

  • f→\overrightarrow{f}f​ is f(x)f(x)f(x)'s coefficients vector.
  • yωN→\overrightarrow{y_{\omega_N}}yωN​​​ is f(x)f(x)f(x)'s outputs vector of f(x)f(x)f(x) evaluated at ωN0,ωN1,...,ωNN−1\omega_N^0, \omega_N^1, ..., \omega_N^{N-1}ωN0​,ωN1​,...,ωNN−1​.
  • DωND_{\omega_N}DωN​​ is a special case of Vandermonde matrix, called the DFT matrix, whose entries can be generated solely from ωN\omega_NωN​ and their positions.

Same as before, we want to convert values in y→\overrightarrow{y}y​ -> coefficients f→\overrightarrow{f}f​, which is esentially the inverse process of FFT.

We have: f→=DωN−1 yωN→\overrightarrow{f} = D_{\omega_N}^{-1} \text{ } \overrightarrow{y_{\omega_N}}f​=DωN​−1​ yωN​​​, where DωND_{\omega_N}DωN​​ is invertible since ωN0≠ωN1≠...≠ωNN−1\omega_N^0 \neq \omega_N^1 \neq ... \neq \omega_N^{N-1}ωN0​=ωN1​=...=ωNN−1​. It turns out that the inverse of a DFT matrix is another DFT matrix scaled down by NNN:

DωN−1=DωN−1ND_{\omega_N}^{-1} = \frac{D_{\omega_N^{-1}}}{N}DωN​−1​=NDωN−1​​​

Since ωN−1\omega_N^{-1}ωN−1​ is also just another root of unity & multiply with a DFT matrix is just doing FFT, this means that the inverse process of FFT (IFFT) is just another FFT with an extra step of scaling down all the outputs by NNN! Ahhh!


What you can take away from this is that IFFT is nearly as fast as FFT (scaling values down by a constant is a really fast process btw). I dunno if there're better methods, so this could be wrong, but if you look at the other method we found to convert values -> coefficients, the difference it has with convert coefficients -> values involves an additional process of inverting a really big matrix: A very complex process that takes a lot of CPU & RAM. So this seems like a huge hidden win that we just discover blindly :-3

Also, this means that if you can somehow make FFT fast, IFFT is also being fast too!


And it turns out there's an optimized version of FFT that allows us to calculate in O(Nlog⁡N)O(N\log{N})O(NlogN), called the Radix-2 FFT! For more details, please consider watching this video by Reducible, this guy is really good :>

Not a Power of 2

In order for this optimized version to work, NNN must be a power of 2 (that's why this is called "Radix-2 FFT"), and 20000156, well, is not. But we can sort of "cheat" by "pretending" that f(x)f(x)f(x) has more than 20000156 terms, with the leading coefficients are all 000s.

f(x)=f0∗x0+f1∗x1+...+fN−1∗xN−1↓f(x)=f0∗x0+f1∗x1+...+fN−1∗xN−1+0∗xN+0∗xN+1+0∗...f(x) = f_0 * x_0 + f_1 * x_1 + ... + f_{N-1} * x_{N-1} \\ \downarrow \\ f(x) = f_0 * x_0 + f_1 * x_1 + ... + f_{N-1} * x_{N-1} + 0 * x_N + 0 * x_{N+1} + 0 * ...f(x)=f0​∗x0​+f1​∗x1​+...+fN−1​∗xN−1​↓f(x)=f0​∗x0​+f1​∗x1​+...+fN−1​∗xN−1​+0∗xN​+0∗xN+1​+0∗...

So why not, say, think that f(x)f(x)f(x) has the number of coefficients NNN = the closet power of two ≥\geq≥ 20000156, idk, like 2252^{25}225?


Can't get much more outputs

But then that would mean we need more than 20000156 outputs!! We might want to use Lagrange Interpolation to recover more values. But it takes O(N2)O(N^2)O(N2)!!

No 2s2^s2s-th roots of unity...

However, even if we're able to extend to some 2s2^s2s length, get some 2s2^s2s outputs, in this case 2252^{25}225. By then we still wouldn't be able to do IFFT: the algorithm requires a 2252^{25}225-th root of unity, and no such thing exists in Fp\mathbb{F}_{p}Fp​.

Because of number theory and group theory stuffs, any subgroup of the multiplicative group Fp×\mathbb{F}_{p}^{\times}Fp×​ must have order dividing p−1p-1p−1. What this is saying is that good luck trying to find g≢1g \not\equiv 1g≡1 such that gx≡1mod  pg^{x} \equiv 1 \mod {p}gx≡1modp when x∤p−1x \nmid p-1x∤p−1... You can start with 3 to experiment 😘

The only prime factors of p−1p - 1p−1 are 2,100000792, 100000792,10000079, we can only use 2,100000792, 100000792,10000079, or 200001582000015820000158-th roots of unity for doing FFT only... But apperantly that means we can only work with cases where NNN = 2, 10000079 or 20000158!?
